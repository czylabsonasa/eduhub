intro

  • for a given set of 2d points (t_{1},f_{1}),\ldots,(t_{n},f_{n}) we are searching the simplest (minimal degree) P polynomial that interpolates the points: P(t_{k})=f_{k}\ \ \ k=1,\ldots ,n.
  • terminology: the elements of t are called interpolation nodes/node points, the elements of f are called (function) values/node values.
  • naturally the point set cannot have two equal first coordinates (t_{i}=t_{j}) with different second coordinates (f_{i}\neq f_{j}).
  • for 1 points a 0-th order, 2 points an at most 1-st order interpolation is clearly exists - but how to proceed to larger number of points?
  • n points n pieces of information. we may expect that these n pieces will determine n real numbers, a polynomial of degree at most n-1, which will be the interpolational polynomial.
  • there are several approaches

    • setting up an n\times n linear system of equations a_{n-1}t_{k}^{n-1}+\ldots+a_{1}t_{k}+a_{0}=f_{k}\ \ \ k=1,\ldots,n

    • applying least square approximation for \varphi_{k}(t)=t^k, \ \ k=0,\ldots,n-1, for if it is possible to achieve the zero error, then the Gauss normal equation will give us the appropriate coefficients.

    • both of these (eventually equivalent) approaches has complexity {\cal O}(n^3) and it is not so straightforward to apply them for a dynamically extending point set.

  • we will learn later that there is a more efficient {\cal O}(n^2) complexity and dynamically extendable approach: the Newton divided difference method.
  • first we will state a theorem about the existence and uniqueness.
ImportantLagrange’s main theorem

For a given set of pairwise different points (t_{1},f_{1}),\ldots,(t_{n},f_{n}), there is a unique polynomial P with: P(t_{k})=f_{k}\ \ \ (k=1,\ldots,n)\ \ \text{and}\ \ \deg(P)\le n-1

  • for more details read this
  • at this stage we learned that the least square approach (even without A^{T}A) will give us the unique solution…

Newton divided difference method

intro

  • search for the polynomial that interpolates the points t=[-1,0,1],\ \ f=[2,3,1].
  • let’s imagine the polynomial in the form of p(x)=a_2(x+1)x+a_1x+a_0. here we included some information about the first coordinates into the polynomial.
Nonedetails

\begin{gather*} p(-1)=2 \ \ \Longrightarrow \ \ a_0=2\\ p(x)=a_2(x+1)x+a_1(x+1)+2\\ p(0)=3 \ \ \Longrightarrow \ \ a_1=1\\ p(x)=a_2(x+1)x+(x+1)+2\\ p(1)=1 \ \ \Longrightarrow \ \ a_2=-\frac{3}{2}\\ p(x)=-\frac{3}{2}(x+1)x+(x+1)+2\\ \end{gather*}

  • observe that at each step we solved a one variable linear equation, as in the case of triangular form linear systems
  • this seemingly ad-hoc approach is transformed to an algorithm

the method (how?)

the scheme of the divided difference method

\begin{array}{c|cccccc} t_1 & f^{~(0)}_{1} & & & & & \\ & & f^{~(1)}_{1} & & & & \\ t_2 & f^{~(0)}_{2} && f^{~(2)}_{1} & & & \\ & & f^{~(1)}_{2} & & \ddots & &\\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & f^{~(n-2)}_{1} & \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & f^{~(n-1)}_{1} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & f^{~(n-2)}_{2} & \\ & & f^{~(1)}_{n-2} & & \mathinner{\kern{1.2mu}\raisebox{2mu}{.}\kern{3mu}\raisebox{7.4mu}{.}\kern{3mu}\raisebox{12.8mu}{.}\kern{1.2mu}}& & \\ t_{n-1} & f^{~(0)}_{n-1} & & f^{~(2)}_{n-2} & & & \\ & & f^{~(1)}_{n-1} & & & & \\ t_n & f^{~(0)}_{n} & & & & & \end{array}

Importantthe computational rule

We start with the t and the f^{~(0)} columns, the the missing entries, for j=1\ldots n-1 and i=1\ldots n-j are computed by f_{i}^{~(j)}=\frac{f_{i}^{~(j-1)}-f_{i+1}^{~(j-1)}}{t_i-t_{i+j}} \ \ \ \ (*)

Importantthe polynomial(s)

Suppose that we filled in the table according to the rule (*). Then \begin{align*} P(t)&=f^{~(0)}_{1}+f^{~(1)}_{1}(t-t_1)+f^{~(2)}_{1}(t-t_1)(t-t_2)+\ldots\\ &+f^{~(n-2)}_{1}(t-t_1)\ldots (t-t_{n-2})+f^{~(n-1)}_{1}(t-t_1)\ldots (t-t_{n-1}) \end{align*} and \begin{align*} P(t)&=f^{~(0)}_{n}+f^{~(1)}_{n-1}(t-t_n)+f^{~(2)}_{n-2}(t-t_n)(t-t_{n-1})+\cdots\\ &+f^{~(n-2)}_{2}(t-t_n)\cdots (t-t_3)+f^{~(n-1)}_{1}(t-t_n)\cdots (t-t_2) \end{align*} are interpolational polynomials for the data t,f.

example

Compute the Lagrange interpolational polynom for the point set:

(-2,-31),(-1,-7),(0,-1),(2,5)

Nonedetails

\begin{array}{r|rrrr} -2 & \color{red} -31 &&&\\ & &\color{red}24 & &\\ -1 & -7 & & \color{red} -9 &\\ & & 6 & & \color{red}2\\ 0& -1 && -1 &\\ && 3 &&\\ 2 & 5 & & & \end{array}

P(t)={\color{red}-31}+{\color{red}24}(t+2){\color{red}-9}(t+2)(t+1)+ {\color{red}2}(t+2)(t+1)t

or reading from the lower edge:

\begin{array}{r|rrrr} -2 & -31 &&&\\ & & 24 & &\\ -1 & -7 & & -9 &\\ & & 6 & & \color{red}2\\ 0& -1 && \color{red}-1 &\\ && \color{red}3 &&\\ 2 & \color{red}5 & & & \end{array}

P(t)={\color{red}5}+{\color{red}3}(t-2){\color{red}-1}(t-2)t+ {\color{red}2}(t-2)t(t+1)

the result in classical form is P(t)=2t^{3}-3t^{2}+t-1 in both cases.

Horner

  • a tiny method to evaluate polynomials

P(t)=a_nt^n+a_{n-1}t^{n-1}+\ldots+a_1t+a_0

  • the prefix polynomials (a_n),\ (a_{n},a_{n-1}),\ \ldots, (a_n,a_{n-1},\ldots,a_1,a_0) evaluated subsequently.
  • suppose that we need the value at s

\begin{align*} v_0&=a_n\\ v_1&=v_0s+a_{n-1}\\ v_2&=v_1s+a_{n-2}\\ \ldots\\ v_n&=v_{n-1}s+a_{0}=P(s)\\ \end{align*}

in tabular form:

\begin{array}{r|cccccc} &a_n & a_{n-1} & \ldots & a_2 & a_1 & a_0 & \\ \hline s & v_0 & v_1 & \ldots & v_{n-2} & v_{n-1} & v_n & =P(s) \\ \end{array}

general Horner

\begin{gather*} P(t)=a_{n-1}(t-t_1)\ldots(t-t_{n-1})+a_{n-2}(t-t_1)\ldots(t-t_{n-2})+\\ \ldots+a_1(t-t_1)+a_0 \end{gather*}

  • the prefix polynomials (a_{n-1}),\ (a_{n-1},a_{n-2}),\ \ldots, (a_{n-1},a_{n-2},\ldots,a_1,a_0) evaluated subsequently.
  • suppose that we need the value at s

\begin{align*} v_0&=a_{n-1}\\ v_1&=v_0(s-t_{n-1})+a_{n-1}\\ v_2&=v_1(s-t_{n-2})+a_{n-2}\\ \ldots\\ v_{n-1}&=v_{n-2}(s-t_{1})+a_{0}=P(s)\\ \end{align*}

in tabular form:

\begin{array}{r|cccccc} & t_{n} & t_{n-1} & \ldots & t_3 & t_2 & t_1 & \\ & a_{n-1} & a_{n-2} & \ldots & a_2 & a_1 & a_0 & \\ \hline s & v_0 & v_1 & \ldots & v_{n-3} & v_{n-2} & v_{n-1} & =P(s)\\ \end{array}

  • note that t_n is unused.

% assumption: t is equidistant (to have O(1) search)
% length(t) is at least 2
% t is increasing
function plin=mk_plin(t,f)
   d=t(2)-t(1);
   a=t(1);
   %b=t(end);
   n=length(t);
   function y=plin0(x)
      k=floor((x-a)/d)+1;
      if k>=n; k=k-1; end % handle when x==b
      y=f(k)+(f(k)-f(k+1))/(t(k)-t(k+1))*(x-t(k));
   end
   plin=@plin0;
end

Hermite-interpolation

  • as we realized there are some issues with the “plain” lagrangian interpolation. If one wants more precise approximation then the number supporting points must be increased, but this could result in increasing “largest deviation” scenario, as we saw for the Runge test-function.
  • it is natural to expect that controlling the derivative values (possibly higher ones) would lead us to a better approximation: the observed fluctuation at the ends of the interval would disappear.

problem description

We have a set of points and a set of conditions at the points: \begin{array}{rcccccl} & t_1 & t_2 & t_3 & \dots & t_n & \\ \hline & f_{1}^{(0)} & f_{2}^{(0)} & f_{3}^{(0)} & \dots & f_{n}^{(0)} &\\ & f_{1}^{(1)} & f_{2}^{(1)} & f_{3}^{(1)} & \dots & f_{n}^{(1)} &\\ & f_{1}^{(2)} & f_{2}^{(2)} & f_{3}^{(2)} & \dots & f_{n}^{(2)} &\\ &\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots &\\ &f_{1}^{(m_1-1)} & f_{2}^{(m_2-1)} & f_{3}^{(m_3-1)} &\dots & f_{n}^{(m_n-1)}&\\ \end{array}

We are looking for a polynomial H(t), for which H^{~(j)}(t_i)=f_{i}^{(j)} \quad \quad i=1,\dots,n, \quad j=0,\dots ,m_i-1. Let m=\sum_{k=1}^n m_k, the number of conditions.

Important

The problem of Hermite-interpolation can be solved uniquely in the space of at most m-1 degree polynomials.

  • From the conditions, we can set up a uniquely solvable m\times m linear system, for the details see this at page (around) 18.
  • or

computation

  • How to compute the coefficients of H?
  • It is possible to modify the Newton divided difference method for Lagrange polynomials to get them.
  • The node points column (t) should contain the t_k’s with multiplicity. As many conditions assigned to a specific as many times we should include it. If the first derivative is given, then we must include it 2 times, and so on.
  • The corresponding computational formula is modified, in order to handle the “division by zero” case
ImportantHermite-rule

f_{i}^{~(j)}= \begin{cases} \frac{f_{i}^{~(j-1)}-f_{i+1}^{~(j-1)}}{t_i-t_{i+j}} \ \ \text{if}\ t_i \neq t_{i+j}\\ \frac{f(t_{i})^{(j)}}{j!} \ \ \text{otherwise}\\ \end{cases}

Important

If the node points are set up with appropriate multiplicity and the modified computational rule used to fill in the values of the scheme then the polynomial in the final table fulfills the conditions. (The proof is omitted.)

applications

tangent line formula

  • For a given real valued, differentiable function find the equation of its tangential line at t_0!
  • We are looking for a Hermite polynomial of degree at most 1, for which H(t_0)=f(t_0) and H~'(t_0)=f~'(t_0)

\begin{array}{r|rr} t_0 & f(t_0) \\ & & f~'(t_0) \\ t_0 & f(t_0) \end{array}

  • then H(t)=f(t_0)+f~'(t_0)(t-t_0)

the Runge-function revisited

  • we will interpolate the function at different node sets, but this time the derivative values will also be used at some nodes.
Nonethe plot

Nonethe script 

clc; clf; clear;
% Runge examples - uniform Hermite

fun=@(x) 1./(1+25*x.^2);
dfun=@(x) -50*x./(1+25*x.^2).^2;
xx=linspace(-1,1);
yy=fun(xx);

nsp=0;
for np=[3,9,15]
  nsp=nsp+1;subplot(3,1,nsp);
  x=linspace(-1,1,np);
  data=cell(1,np);
  % also control some derivatives at the ends
  data{1}=[x(1),fun(x(1)),dfun(x(1))];
  data{2}=[x(2),fun(x(2)),dfun(x(2))];
  for i=3:np-2
    data{i}=[x(i),fun(x(i))];
  end
  data{np-1}=[x(np-1),fun(x(np-1)),dfun(x(np-1))];
  data{np}=[x(np),fun(x(np)),dfun(x(np))];
  
  [H,t]=hermite_interpolation(data);
  yyy=horner_newton(H,t,xx);

  plot(xx,yy,xx,yyy);
  maxdiff=max(abs(yy-yyy));
  title(sprintf("uniform+Hermite | num of points: %d | max difference: %.2f\n",np,maxdiff));
end

piecewise interpolation

  • the large interval is divided into smaller pieces and low-order polynomials will be used

error of the linear interpolation

  • let f\in C^{2}[A,B] and M_2=\max_{t\in[A,B]} |f{~''}(t)|
  • then for the linear interpolation P of f on [a,b]\subset [A,B] we have:
Important

\vert f(t)-P_{[a,b]}(t)\vert \leq \frac {M_2}{8}(b-a)^2, \quad\quad t\in [a,b]

  • Where .
  • increasing the number of subintervals results in smaller error.
  • dividing the “large” [A,B] interval int n equal pieces (and using the same “global” M_2) the error for one [a,b] subinterval:

\vert f(t)-P_{[a,b]}\vert \leq \frac {M_2}{8}\frac{(B-A)^2}{n^2}, \quad\quad t\in [a,b]

  • doubling the points = halving the length will result 4 times better approximation
  • pro: simple
  • con: sometimes for very high precision we need large number of points, but at least it works

higher-order interpolation

  • one can use plain Lagrange or Hermite interpolation for the small intervals
  • pro: better approximation (worst case)
  • con: computationally intensive, harder to implement

cubic spline interpolation

  • the most widely used piecewise interpolation method
  • given [t_1,\ldots,t_n] and [f_1,\ldots,f_n] and f_1' and f'_n
  • the first derivatives are given only the endpoints
  • We search for a function S(t) for which
    • S(t_k)=f_k
    • S'(t_1)=f_1' and S'(t_n)=f'_n
    • S|_{[t_{k},t_{k+1}]}=S_k cubic polynomial, =1,\dots ,n-1
    • S is twice continuously differentiable
  • the are several approaches to construct the sub-polynomials. We do not discuss them.

matlab

  • the spline function can be used
  • not supplying the derivatives some (implementation dependent) defaults will be used
  • spline(t, [df1 f dfn], x) will evalute the polynomial at x
  • a Hermite problem t_1,t_2,f_1,f_2,f'_1,f'_2 is cubic spline problem for one-interval. it can be solved by a simple call of spline. (at least the evaluation part)
